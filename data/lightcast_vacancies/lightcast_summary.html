Overview

Occupational vacancy rates are constructed by combining two data sources: job posting microdata from Lightcast, which provides monthly counts of full-time vacancy postings by occupation for the between January 2010 and March 2024, and employment stock estimates from the Bureau of Labor Statistics’ Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) programme, which provides annual employment counts by occupation. Vacancy rates are defined as the monthly count of full-time postings in a given occupation divided by the annual employment level in that occupation in the same calendar year. The construction of this measure requires substantial data processing across three stages: first, aggregating the raw Lightcast microdata into a consistent monthly occupational panel; second, building a matched annual employment series that reconciles differences in occupational classification systems and coverage across the two sources; and, third, calculating an occupation-level vacancy rate at the annual and monthly levels. Lightcast classifies occupations using the Standard Occupational Classification (SOC). The following process yields vacancy rates at the minor group SOC-3 (n ~ 100; reliably), broad SOC-4 (n ~ 440; possible but requires a lot of assumptions using occupational crosswalks), and detailed SOC-5 (n ~ 800; not advisable) levels.

Step 1: Processing the Lightcast Vacancy Microdata

The raw Lightcast data contains observations of individual job posting with attributes including occupation, employment type, and posting date. We only consider full-time postings and exclude duplicates using the Lightcast methodology. We aggregate these sub-files to monthly observations to produce a single vacancy count per SOC code per month by summing the count of full-time vacancies within each SOC code. The final output is one long-format panel with month-SOC level (SOC-3, SOC-4, and SOC-5) observations, covering all occupation-month observations from 2010 to 2024.

Step 2: Matching Vacancy Data to Employment Levels for Vacancy Rates

To construct the vacancy rates, we match the occupational codes present in the Lightcast data to their employment levels as reported in OEWS. Matching the Lightcast vacancy panel to OEWS employment data is complicated by three sources of discontinuity: (i) the OEWS did not publish occupational aggregates at the minor-group (SOC-3) or broad-group (SOC-4) levels in 2010 and 2011, reporting only major-group (SOC-2) totals and (SOC-5&6) individual detailed occupations in those years; (ii) the 2018 SOC revision renumbered and reorganised several occupational groups, creating breaks in the code series; and (iii) the OEWS survey excludes certain industries and occupational categories (most notably agriculture, fishing, the military, and self-employment) meaning some SOC codes present in the Lightcast data have no corresponding OEWS counterpart. Each issue is addressed through a distinct strategy, applied in a strict priority ordering so that the most reliable data source is always preferred.

For the 2010–2011 gap at the SOC-3 level, minor-group employment totals are reconstructed by summing the broad-group sub-codes (or, where broad codes are absent, the detailed codes) that fall within each minor group, identified via a 4-character SOC prefix match. For three codes affected by the 2018 SOC revision () 15-1200 (Computer Occupations), 31-1100 (Home Health and Personal Care Aides), and 19-5000 (Occupational Health and Safety)) the predecessor minor-group code from the 2010 SOC taxonomy is substituted directly for the years 2012–2018, as these represent straightforward renumberings with no change in occupational scope. A special case arises for 19-5000, which was moved across major groups (from major group 29, Healthcare, to major group 19, Life and Physical Science) in the 2018 revision: because the predecessor detailed codes (29-9011 and 29-9012) share no prefix with the new code, the standard prefix-based construction would erroneously return missing values for 2010–2011. These years are therefore handled by an explicit crosswalk that names the predecessor detailed codes directly and sums them. Three codes are treated as entirely unresolvable: 45-3000 (Fishing and Hunting Workers), which lost OEWS coverage after the 2017 estimates due to the exclusion of the fishing industry from the OEWS sampling frame; 55-9000 (Military); 99-9000 (Unclassified). Each observation in the final matched employment series carries a source indicator (direct_match, predecessor_code, or constructed) recording which strategy was used, allowing sensitivity analyses to be conducted on subsets matched via any single method.
